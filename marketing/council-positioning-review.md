# Marketing Council Review — Positioning & Market Awareness

> *Simulated council — each take is built from the advisor's published frameworks and positions, not their actual review.*

---

## The question before the council

AWSM is a 16-year-old Polish-German IT nearshore company serving German insurance, fintech, financial services, and ERP/SAP clients. Their positioning bets on **"communication is as important as technology"** — they screen for German/English fluency and soft skills equally with tech stack fit, differentiating against larger Polish nearshore firms (Sii, Luxoft) and indirect alternatives (German local hiring, DIY Polish subsidiary, Ukrainian/Indian outsourcing).

**What's at stake:** AWSM is refining its GTM messaging across four verticals. The positioning document and ICP profile have been drafted. Before investing in campaigns, the council is asked: are the differentiators real and defensible? Is the market awareness correctly estimated? And is the messaging over-indexing on rational B2B logic at the expense of psychological differentiation?

**Seated:** April Dunford (positioning), Eugene Schwartz (market awareness & sophistication), Rory Sutherland (designated dissenter — behavioral/irrational).

---

### April Dunford — Positioning against real alternatives

*If your product didn't exist, what would your customer honestly do instead?*

AWSM passes this test partially. The ICP correctly identifies the alternatives: German local hiring, larger Polish nearshore firms, Ukrainian/Romanian nearshore, DIY Polish subsidiary, freelancer platforms. That's honest and specific — most B2B companies stop at "competitor X."

But here's where I'd push: you've identified **five alternatives**, which means five different positioning frames depending on which one the prospect is actually comparing you to. A CTO comparing you to hiring in Munich needs a different pitch than a Technical Lead comparing you to Sii or Luxoft. Your current positioning statement ("Polish-German IT teams with communication in equal measure") tries to work against all five at once. It's too generic.

My framework says: pick the **most dangerous alternative** — the one you lose to most often — and position directly against it. From your ICP, that seems to be **"do nothing / keep struggling with local hiring"** (status quo) and **"try another large Polish nearshore firm"** (direct competitor). These require different positioning:

- **Against status quo:** You're not selling "Polish-German teams." You're selling *the difference between a project that ships in 2027 and one that ships in 2026*. The alternative is continued hiring paralysis — 137k unfilled roles in Germany.

- **Against Sii/Luxoft:** Here your communication story is actually a real differentiator. But I'd test whether it's *provable*. "We screen for soft skills" is a process claim, not a customer-verifiable outcome. What's the proof? Lower turnover? Faster ramp-up time? Shorter time-to-fluency in the client's codebase? You need an *outcome* the customer can see, not a process they have to trust.

**Your biggest risk:** "Communication" as a differentiator is hard to own because every competitor will claim it. The market is sophisticated enough (Schwartz will address this) that claims about "great communication" are table stakes — they don't differentiate. Your actual differentiator might be something you haven't centered yet: **16 years of insurance-specific domain knowledge in Polish-German context** — that's a moat. Communication is the enabler, but domain expertise is the differentiator.

**Bottom line:** Narrow your positioning to one primary competitive alternative per vertical. Lead with domain expertise (insurance/ERP/16 years), use communication as the supporting proof, not the headline. And give sales an outcome-based claim, not a process claim.

### Eugene Schwartz — Market awareness & sophistication

Let me apply my two frameworks: awareness stage and market sophistication.

**Awareness stage of your typical prospect:**

Your ICP suggests prospects are somewhere between **Problem-Aware** and **Solution-Aware**:

- They know they can't hire developers in Germany (Problem-Aware).
- Some know nearshoring to Poland is a solution (Solution-Aware).
- Very few are AWSM-Aware (Product-Aware) — they don't know you exist until they research.

This means your headlines and opening moves **cannot lead with your name, your differentiators, or your "communication story."** That's Product-Aware copy aimed at a Problem-Aware audience. It will bounce.

Your home page currently opens with: *"Reliable technology and clear communication are fundamental essentials — For 16 years, we have been building Polish-German IT teams."* That's a Solution-Aware to Product-Aware headline. For the first-time visitor who is Problem-Aware ("we can't hire"), it assumes too much. They don't care about "Polish-German IT teams" yet — they care about the gap in their delivery timeline.

The right starting headline for a Problem-Aware prospect would be: *"137,000 IT positions in Germany are unfilled. Yours doesn't have to be one of them."* Then: "Here's how German insurance leaders are solving it." Then: "With AWSM." You have to move them through Problem → Solution → Product, not skip steps.

**Market sophistication:**

This is where I'm most concerned. Your market — German IT procurement for insurance/finance — is **highly sophisticated** (Stage 4 or 5 on my scale). They have been hearing claims from nearshore providers for 20+ years. Everything has been claimed:

- "High quality" — claimed by everyone.
- "Great communication" — claimed by everyone.
- "Cost savings" — claimed by everyone.
- "Cultural fit" — claimed by everyone.

When a market reaches Stage 4-5, the prospect has heard every claim so many times that they've become deaf to them. The mechanism that still works at this stage is one of two things: either **elaborate the mechanism** ("Here's *how* we get German-fluent Java developers that integrate in 2 weeks — a proprietary 5-step vetting process") or **shift to identification** ("You're a CTO at a German insurance company who's tired of being the bottleneck between your board's expectations and your team's capacity. We're built for that exact tension.").

Your current messaging leads with the claim ("communication + technology") — which the market has heard before. You need to move to mechanism, and then to identification.

**Specifically for each vertical:**

- **Insurance:** Sophistication is highest here. Move straight to mechanism (the vetting process, the compliance package) or identification ("Built for insurance IT leaders who can't afford another failed outsourcing experiment").
- **Fintech:** Slightly less sophisticated. Claims still have some power, especially with a new mechanism attached.
- **ERP/SAP:** Moderate sophistication. The S/4HANA deadline gives you a natural timing hook that resets sophistication — you're not selling nearshoring, you're selling "2027 migration capability."
- **Financial services:** As sophisticated as insurance. Same approach.

**Bottom line:** You are writing at the wrong awareness stage — your copy assumes Product-Awareness when most prospects are Problem-Aware. And your claims land in a sophisticated market that has heard them all. The solution is not better claims — it's mechanism or identification. Lead with the mechanism of how you're different, not the claim that you are.

### Rory Sutherland — Designated dissenter

*"Most marketing problems are perception problems, not reality problems." — Alchemy*

April and Eugene have given you excellent rational analysis. That's exactly the problem. You're competing in a market where every player is optimizing on the same rational dimensions — quality, speed, cost, communication — and converging on the same language. That's the doorman fallacy in action: you've defined your job so narrowly ("provide developers with German skills") that you're missing the psychological dimensions where real leverage sits.

Let me give you three counterintuitive moves to consider:

**1. The price signal is wrong.** You're positioning as "35-50% cheaper than German hiring." This is rational and true, but it signals *cheapness*. In German enterprise procurement, cheap triggers suspicion, not attraction. "How can they be good if they're half the price?" The BearingPoint study confirms this — quality concerns are one of the top barriers to outsourcing. What if you priced at 80-90% of German rates — not 50% — and competed entirely on speed and domain expertise? You'd lose the price-sensitive tire-kickers (good) and signal confidence to the serious buyers. The cost savings would still be real, but you wouldn't lead with them. You'd mention them only after establishing competence.

**2. The category frame is wrong.** You call yourself "IT nearshoring." That's a procurement category — it signals cost arbitrage, commodity talent, high management overhead. What if you called yourselves something that signals the opposite? "German Insurance Development Partners." "Polish Engineering Studios for DACH." A name that makes a CTO feel *smart* for choosing you, not *frugal*. Expensive signals work in B2B too — the cost of the engagement itself communicates something about its seriousness.

**3. The psychological problem isn't "we can't hire developers."** That's the rational problem. The psychological problem is: *"I'm a CTO whose career depends on a project I can't staff. Every month I fail to deliver makes me look bad to the Vorstand. I've tried outsourcing before and it was a nightmare. I'm terrified of making the wrong choice."* Your current messaging addresses the rational layer (cost, quality, communication) but not the emotional one (career risk, fear of repeating past failures, desire to look competent).

Address the emotional layer directly: *"We know you've been burned before. Here's why our clients stay 7+ years."* That's not a claim — it's an understanding. It signals that you get the *real* problem.

**Counterintuitive test:** What if your headline was *not* about technology or communication, but about *disappointment avoidance*? "We help insurance CTOs sleep better at night." It sounds soft, but it addresses the actual emotional tension — and it's memorable. Anything you test, test the absurd version too. The opposite of a good idea can also be a good idea.

**Bottom line:** You're over-indexing on rational persuasion in a market where the rational arguments have converged. The highest-leverage move is probably not a better claim — it's a different *signal*. What does your price, your category name, and your headline say about you *before* anyone reads a word of copy?

## Where the council disagrees

### 1. Dunford vs. Schwartz: Which alternative to position against?

**Dunford** says pick the *most dangerous competitive alternative* (likely status quo or a specific named competitor like Sii/Luxoft) and position head-to-head against it. Positioning must be narrow to be strong.

**Schwartz** says the prospect's awareness stage dictates the frame — most prospects are Problem-Aware, not Solution-Aware, so they aren't even comparing alternatives yet. Positioning against a competitor assumes awareness they don't have.

**The real trade-off:** Do you optimize for the 20% of prospects who are solution-shopping (Dunford's frame) or the 80% who are still stuck in the problem (Schwartz's frame)? For AWSM, the answer probably depends on channel: inbound content from search (Clutch, LinkedIn) catches solution-aware prospects; outbound (cold email, events) catches problem-aware ones. Both frames are needed but in different channels. The mistake would be picking one and calling it done.

### 2. Schwartz vs. Sutherland: Rational mechanism vs. psychological identification

**Schwartz** says in a sophisticated market (Stage 4-5), the mechanism is what still works — "Here's *how* we build German-fluent teams." Elaborate the process, the vetting, the compliance.

**Sutherland** says the mechanism is just more rational competition; the real leverage is psychological. Signal, identification, and counterintuitive framing beat mechanism in a converged market.

**The real trade-off:** A 5-step vetting process is easy to copy and commoditizes you. But pure psychological play ("sleep better at night") lacking substance is just fluff. The middle path: wrap the mechanism in a frame that signals confidence. Example: "We spent 16 years perfecting one thing: Polish-German teams for insurance. Here's what that means for your S/4HANA migration." That's mechanism + identification + a costly signal (16 years of focus).

### 3. Dunford vs. Sutherland: Is "communication" a real differentiator?

**Dunford** questions whether "communication" is provable and defensible — every competitor claims it, and it's hard to demonstrate in a sales pitch.

**Sutherland** would say "communication" fails because it's a rational claim in a rational category. The real signal isn't what you *say* about communication — it's what your operations *cost* and what your category *name* implies.

**The real trade-off:** Dunford would solve this by finding a more provable differentiator (domain expertise, 16 years, insurance specialization). Sutherland would solve it by reframing the entire category. Both agree the current "communication" claim is too weak — they just disagree on the fix. The practical answer: keep communication as the *reason* you deliver outcomes, but not the headline. Lead with domain expertise and speed; use communication as the supporting mechanism.

## Chair's synthesis

All three advisors converge on a central problem: **AWSM's positioning is too generic for the market's sophistication level and too rational for its emotional stakes.** The fixes:

### Do:

1. **Restructure headlines by awareness stage.** Problem-Aware prospects (majority) need: problem → mechanism → product. Not product first. Move your homepage from "We build Polish-German IT teams" to "137k unfilled IT roles in Germany — here's how insurance leaders are solving it."

2. **Replace "communication" as the primary differentiator with something more provable.** Best candidates: *domain expertise* (16 years insurance/finance), *speed* (candidate suggestion in days, not months), or *retention* (7+ year client relationships, low turnover). Use communication and soft-skill screening as the *reason* you achieve these outcomes — the "how," not the "what."

3. **Add emotional layer to sales messaging.** Every CTO prospect has been burned before. Address fear of failure and career risk explicitly. "We know outsourcing can go wrong. Here's why our clients trust us — and why they stay 7+ years."

4. **Test a price frame shift.** Current: "35-50% cheaper than German hiring." Test: "We don't compete on price. We compete on speed and domain expertise. The fact that we happen to be more cost-effective is a side effect, not the reason to choose us." See which generates better meetings.

5. **Per vertical: tune awareness stage and sophistication separately.** Insurance and banking are highly sophisticated (Stage 4-5) — lead with mechanism or identification, never claims. Fintech is less sophisticated (Stage 3-4) — claims still work if backed by mechanism. ERP/SAP has a natural "reset" via the 2027 migration deadline — exploit it.

### Tripwire:

Watch for the signal that your positioning is still too generic: **prospects say "we'll think about it" instead of "tell me more."** That's the Dunford alarm — you haven't made the alternative costly enough to force a decision. When that happens, sharpen the alternative: "If you don't fill these roles in the next 3 months, what happens to your Q4 deliverables?"

### Execute with:

- **copywriting** — rewrite homepage and landing page headlines following Schwartz's awareness-stage structure
- **product-marketing** — refine the differentiator from "communication" to domain expertise + speed (update `.agents/product-marketing.md`)
- **cro** — A/B test the "cheaper vs. faster vs. domain expert" frames against each other
- **cold-email** — apply emotional layer (fear of failure, career risk) to outbound sequences
